International people in these times of crisis, no one pauses to think about them. Americans are doing the American thing. The Dutch are doing that. The German government is doing what is beneficial to the Germans. There is no global approach to this crisis.



Kenneth, Kenyan-national based in Amsterdam

In March 2020 a global pandemic was proclaimed and countries shut down their borders in an effort to contain the spread of the virus. Like for Savannah and Malte, the virus put international couples to the test. These are some of their stories.


Globalization has boosted the number of international couples around the world. In the U.S., Dan and Nancy are one of them.

In New Mexico, where the two live, it was at the earlier stages of the virus. The governor had just prohibited gatherings of more than 10 people and with new announcements coming in daily no one could be sure how long registries would remain open.

When the courthouse official cancelled, Dan and Nancy had to think fast knowing they didn’t want to push back the wedding. Firstly, they liked the date, a Friday, and that it represented the beginning of spring. But mostly they were eager to start the immigration process for Nancy, a Mexican national, and to legalize their partnership in the U.S.

Just in time before the quarantine measures set in, Dan and Nany rearranged their plans and got married outside a park, in the presence of the minister, two witnesses and a handful of friends.

With restaurants closed they skipped the romantic dinner they had planned, grabbed a slice of Pizza at Costco (Nancy's favourite) and decided to stock up for the lockdown instead. Given the circumstances the couple found their own way of embracing the situation.



This corona thing really messed up all of our plans. Everyone was stressed out, because it was the first week with the first few cases in New Mexico. So, we went to the Mexican barrio and bought a piñata, a corona piñata and got a bunch of candy and toilet paper inside the corona and beat the hell out of it.


While Dan and Nancy were lucky enough to celebrate with a few friends present at the ceremony, elsewhere couples had to adjust to tighter social-distancing measures.




A few weeks into Trump’s travel ban, Germany imposed its own ban against a number of European countries, including Switzerland, where Jonas and Maria live. Getting to Germany, Jonas' homeland, wasn’t so much the problem, they qualified for essential travel . But the country had limited gatherings to a maximum of two people. Counting the couple, the translator and the registrar, the status of their civil wedding was pending.

For a German couple postponing their civil wedding is a fairly straightforward process. Gathering a few documents, like passport, birth certificate and registration card usually do the trick. Restarting the entire procedure for Maria, however, was a painful thought.


We waited almost a year to get all our documents sorted", explained Maria, who comes from Lebanon.

Documents had to be translated and each piece of paper accredited by the German embassy in Beirut.

Relinquishing the presence of her family who was stuck in Lebanon was hard enough. But postponing would have been a bureaucratic nightmare.

When the two eventually married at the end of March, they had to adhere to Germany’s new rules of ‘Kontaktverbot’. Just the couple was allowed inside the registry and a glass divided them from the registrar.

Even before the crisis, parents of international couples had to adjust to the reality of having to keep in touch with loved ones through their phones. As the virus started to creep up globally, parents and friends had an old acquaintance to rely on: a functioning internet connection.


Like Maria’s and Nancy’s family, following their daughter’s wedding through video call was the best Meghan’s parents could do. Meghan had left the U.K. in 2016 and came to work as a primary school teacher in Kenya, where she met Ronjey, a music teacher who taught at the same school in Nairobi.

At 6am in the morning, U.K. time, Meghan got her dad, mum and 3 of her friends, all spread throughout the country from Wartling to Oxford on video call. In true British fashion, they watched with their cups of tea - her mum had a glass of wine - as their daughter and friend got married in Siyaa, a few meters away where Ronjey was born.

As much as the wedding was a celebration for the newlywed couple, it was also a distraction for Megan’s family back in the U.K. “I think we made quite a few people’s day.”, Meghan said. “In the UK at that moment when everything was getting more and more unsettling and starting to go into lockdown, jobs stopped paying, to be able to send them all these photos, everyone was like: you made our day.”

Despite the complications, Meghan and Ranjey were happy with how their ceremony went about. “We were looking forward to celebrating with family and friends, but ultimately it shows you that you’re doing it to get married rather than to have a big party”, Meghan says.


The corona crisis has challenged international couples in unseen ways as countries put their national interest and putting the well-being of their nationals first. Added government bureaucracy and travel restrictions left many only with the option to postpone.

With months of careful planning compromised in a day postponing can be frustrating. At first they were angry, says Antonello, an Italian based in Brussels who meant to marry his fiancé in Czech Republic this spring.

This initial reaction changed after a while. “In this situation, I think you just need to understand that there's nothing you can really do. It's really beyond your control. There are so many people who are actually suffering much more than a young couple who wants to get married. ”

But putting a pause on wedding plans can have bigger implications than just missing out on a party with friends and family. Many need to legalize their partnership to apply for citizenship for instance.

The corona virus has changed the pace of live and will do so for the unforeseeable future. Couples have succumbed to this new sense of uncertainty not knowing how to plan when information about the virus changes seemingly every day.

With the lessons learned during the pandemic and the heavy toll the crisis has put on especially those not in a position to stay together, it has shifted priorities. For the majority it meant adjusting their plans. But for some couples it made them realize they want to get married sooner.

“I think the situation has made us want to get married because we have different passports and you know, being married legally, would make things easier if you're in the same country and have more rights in each other's countries”, says Simran, a Thai national.

The experience of being separated and not enjoying the same rights can change things, like for Simran who has not seen her boyfriend since she left the U.K. and returned to Bangkok, because her visa was expiring.

“Don't get me wrong. We both want to be together and get married anyway, but I think it would have been different if we both had the same nationality.”
